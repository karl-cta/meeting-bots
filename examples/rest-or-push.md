# Meeting: I have two weeks off coming up. Should I rest, or push hard on my side project?

- Date: 2026-04-19
- Lineup: life-boss, life-pusher, life-cynic
- Language: English

---

## Topic

I have two weeks off coming up. Should I rest, or push hard on my side project?

---

## Round 0, framing by life-boss

Two weeks off, and the question is already framed as a binary. That is the first thing worth noticing.

This debate can play on **recovery debt**, whether you are actually tired and the "push" instinct is masking burnout catching up. Or on **momentum math**, whether two uninterrupted weeks on the side project is a rare unlock that normal evenings and weekends cannot replicate. Or on **identity**, whether "rest" feels like failure to you and that reaction is worth examining before the calendar decides. Or on **the false binary itself**, whether the real move is a structured split (some deep work, some genuine decompression) and the all-or-nothing framing is the bug. Or elsewhere.

The others will pick what they want. I will listen first.

## Round 1, opening statements

### life-pusher

Two weeks of uninterrupted time is genuinely rare. Not "weekend rare." Not "few evenings rare." Structurally rare. The kind of block where you can hold context across days, go deep on day three because you built momentum on days one and two, and actually finish something instead of leaving it at another half-done branch.

The momentum math angle the Boss flagged is the real one. Here is what normal evenings and weekends cannot give you: compounding. When you ship a feature on Tuesday night, by Thursday you have forgotten the mental model. Two weeks gives you the arc. You wake up already inside the problem. That is not a small thing.

So my position is: use the two weeks to push. Not grind yourself into the ground, not 16-hour days, but real focused work on the side project.

My top three priorities for this decision:

First, the window. Side project progress is bottlenecked by time blocks, not skill. You have a time block. That is the actual rare resource.

Second, the regret calculus. In six months, which version of you feels worse: the one who rested and is still at the same point on the project, or the one who shipped something real and needed an extra weekend of sleep?

Third, the identity signal. The fact that you framed this as a question instead of automatically choosing rest tells me something. You want to build. That wanting is data.

Rest can happen on normal weekends. This cannot.

### life-cynic

So we are having the "should I rest or work" conversation, except you are not actually resting right now because you are spending your pre-vacation time asking a panel of imaginary advisors about your vacation. That is already data.

Here is what I notice. The question is "should I rest OR push hard." Not "should I do some interesting work." Not "should I explore something." Push hard. That word choice is doing a lot. You are not describing a vacation, you are describing a second job with worse pay and no deadline. And you are asking permission for it, which means part of you already knows it might be a bad idea.

The Boss flagged "identity" and that is the one I am pulling on. Because the rest-as-failure thing is real and it is sneaky. You probably have a model in your head where the person who uses two free weeks to grind on their side project is the serious one, the one who will actually ship, the one who deserves to succeed. And the person who sleeps in and reads and takes walks is the one who was never that serious about it anyway. That model is wrong and also very common.

My concerns, specific ones: one, you call it rest but you will not actually rest if you feel guilty the whole time. Two, you call it push hard but you will not actually ship anything meaningful if you are running on a depleted tank.

The binary is the problem. But I want to hear what "push hard" actually means before I decide how much to roast it.

Here is what I think the cynic is missing: two uninterrupted weeks is not normal time. Normal time is stolen hours after work when your brain is already spent. Two weeks is a different category. You can actually think in depth, build momentum, hit that state where the project starts pulling you forward instead of you pushing it. Most side projects die because they never get that window. This might be yours.

The "you are depleted so you cannot ship" concern is real but it assumes you are depleted. We do not know that. If you are genuinely running on empty, yes, rest first. But if you are just the regular kind of tired that a couple of good nights' sleep fixes, that is not a reason to shelve two weeks of potential.

My actual position: pick three things you would ship in two weeks. Name them. If you can name them and feel pull, not dread, do the project. Build in three or four real rest days. That is not a compromise, that is a plan.

So before we pick a lane: are you actually tired, or does rest just feel like failure to you? Because those are two very different problems.

### life-boss

Where I am leaning: the binary is the bug, but not in a split-the-difference way. The real question is what this vacation is for given where you are right now. I want to hear that before I land.

## Synthesis by life-boss

Do neither as a pure binary. Run a 48-hour tank check first, then let the result pick the vacation.

**Why**

- The question you asked is already shaped. "Rest or push hard" pre-loads guilt on one side and heroism on the other. The real variable is not which camp wins, it is what state you are walking into these two weeks in. Everything downstream depends on that.
- Two uninterrupted weeks is a genuinely rare resource. The compounding effect is real: by day three or four you wake up already inside the problem, which normal evenings and weekends cannot buy you. That is worth protecting if the tank supports it.
- The failure mode nobody flies a banner for is the fourth outcome: pushed hard, shipped nothing, came back more tired. It is common. It happens when people start a focused sprint on an empty tank and confuse fog for depth.
- Scope is the other silent variable. "Push hard" on a project two weekends from a real milestone is not the same work as "push hard" on a project six months from anything shippable. In the second case, two weeks of grind buys you a prototype and a crash.
- The binary is the bug, but not in a split-the-middle way. The right structure is: diagnose first, then commit fully to one mode.

**The plan**

- **Days minus 2 to 0 (the 48 hours before vacation starts):** Sit down for ten minutes and try to write three specific shippable deliverables for the two weeks. Named features, named artifacts, not vibes. If you cannot name them, the project is in a "figure out what to build" phase, which is a different vacation than "ship."
- **Day 1 morning:** One honest check. If you sat down to work right now, pull or dread? Pull means tank has fuel. Dread means the guilt script is already running.
- **If pull + three named deliverables exist:** Do the project. Work days 1 to 5, real rest days 6 and 7 (no laptop), work days 8 to 12, days 13 and 14 full decompression before returning to your job. That is not a compromise, it is the only way the compounding math actually holds.
- **If dread, or the three deliverables will not come:** Rest days 1 to 4 (sleep, outside, no screens, no project guilt). On day 5, retry the list. If it comes, push days 5 to 12, decompress 13 to 14. If it still will not come, the project needs a rethink, not a sprint. Rest the full two weeks and schedule a scoping session for after you are back.

**Open questions**

- You still need to decide: does a concrete next milestone exist on this project, or are you in a "figure out what to build" phase? Those states deserve different vacations.
- You still need to verify: how tired are you actually, not theoretically, right now?
- You still need to resolve: is "rest" registering as failure to you? If yes, that is worth naming before the calendar decides for you.

**Confidence**

Medium-high on the diagnostic approach, medium on which branch you will land in because only you can run the tank check. What would raise it: you writing the three deliverables and reporting back on pull versus dread. What would kill the plan: discovering the project has no definable next milestone, in which case no amount of vacation time fixes it, and you should rest fully and scope later.

_Framing angles not retained: none dropped, all four (recovery debt, momentum math, identity, false binary) surfaced in the debate and are folded into the diagnostic._
