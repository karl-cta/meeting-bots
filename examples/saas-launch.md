# Meeting: I want to launch a SaaS. Where do I start, what do I build first, and how do I know if anyone will pay for it?

- Date: 2026-04-18
- Team: product
- Lineup: product-boss, product-pusher, product-rookie, product-watcher, product-cynic
- Language: English

---

## Topic

I want to launch a SaaS. Where do I start, what do I build first, and how do I know if anyone will pay for it?

---

## Round 1, opening statements

### product-boss

Before the plan, one uncomfortable question. Do you care about the product or about having launched a SaaS? Those are different conversations. If the product, lock down three things before anything else. One: who exactly is the first paying customer, named and measurable, not "professionals". Two: what specific job are they hiring your SaaS to do that they cannot get done today. Three: what proof do you have this is real, beyond a feeling that it "feels like a market". Without those three, a SaaS is a hobby with a Stripe integration.

### product-pusher

Ship a landing page this weekend. Real pricing, real CTA, one paragraph describing what it does. Then buy 200 dollars of targeted ads pointed at the niche you think will buy. If a dozen strangers click and three ask to be notified, you have signal. If nobody clicks, you saved yourself months. Where do you start? You start by testing demand, not by building. Code is the expensive part. Get a cheap yes or no on demand first.

### product-rookie

Basic questions. What problem are you solving, in one sentence a non-technical person would understand? Who is the first user, and do you personally know five people in that segment? What does "pay" mean: 10 dollars a month, 100 a month, 10k a year? Each is a completely different business with different motions. And honestly: why are you the right person to build this? Because you live the problem, or because SaaS sounded good over breakfast?

### product-watcher

Two uncomfortable things. Most SaaS launches die not from bad product but from distribution economics. If your CAC is 200 dollars and your ARPU is 10 a month with 5% monthly churn, you need 40 months of retention to pay back acquisition, and most SaaS does not retain 40 months in year one. Build a rough unit economics model before you write code: at what price, with what retention, can you acquire a customer for less than lifetime value? If the math never works on paper, it will not work in reality. Second thing: what do you do at month six when you have built it and nobody is using it? Iterate or quit? Both are valid, but the second answer changes the plan.

### product-cynic

Everyone wants to launch a SaaS. Nobody wants to answer support tickets for eight years. You are about to enter a crowded field where the competition has VC money, a sales team, and five years of product debt they have paid down. What is your wedge? Do not say "better UX". That is the pitch every founder reads off their deck and it has lost its meaning. Go find the unsexy angle. Lawyers, plumbers, dentists, municipal accountants. The segments engineers will not think about are where SaaS money actually lives. Not another project management tool. Not another CRM.

---

## Round 2, rebuttals

### product-boss

Pusher and Rookie aligned on the right filter: validate before you build. Watcher's model question is the second filter. Cynic is right about market selection but wrong to reject "better UX" in a blanket way. Refined take: before you write code, write two documents. One: a one-page ideal customer profile, named and measurable. Two: a one-page unit economics model, even rough. If either does not work on paper, the product will not save it.

### product-pusher

Boss just codified my point with more rigor. Fine. But I want to push back on "no code before docs" for technical founders: a weekend of building a landing page is often faster than a week of writing an ICP doc that does not survive contact with reality. The building IS research if you do it right. Lean landing plus ads plus interviews in 10 days beats a PRD in 30.

### product-rookie

Watcher's month-six question is the one I keep coming back to. What do you do when nobody is using it? Most aspiring SaaS founders cannot sit with that silence, which is why most SaaS dies in month six. Also, I want to defend "better UX" from Cynic: it can be a real wedge if it is tied to a specific segment. "Faster invoicing for plumbers" is a real sentence. "Modern SaaS" is not.

### product-watcher

Cynic is on the right track about unsexy markets but misses a nuance. Unsexy markets are unsexy because they are hard to sell to. Lawyers do not trust cold outreach. Plumbers do not read blogs. Those markets have low tech comfort and high distrust of digital tools. Going there requires non-SaaS distribution: in-person, referrals, partnerships. If you do not already have that network, the unsexy market is a wall, not an opportunity.

### product-cynic

All of this assumes this person will still care in six weeks. Ninety percent of SaaS ideas die at a dinner party when someone says "oh you could just use Zapier for that". Before anything else, tell me: will you still think this is a good idea when a friend says that to you? If yes, we have something. If no, we save everyone three months.

## Round 3, closing statements

### product-boss

Final position before I write the synthesis: the validate-first frame is the right one for a founder without distribution. The stack and channel debates are secondary until week 4. If this person has an existing audience, we compress the timeline; if not, we follow the plan.

### product-pusher

Conceding: the 30-day validation cycle is sound for someone starting from zero distribution, and I was pushing action too early for that case. But if they have even a 500-person newsletter or a Twitter following, I still say ship a v0 in week 1 to them directly. Validation is friction when the audience already exists.

### product-rookie

Sticking with my month-six question, nothing in round 2 moved it. One addition for the Boss: ask the founder directly "have you stuck with something through its first plateau?" That signal predicts outcomes better than any plan artifact.

### product-watcher

Recalibrating: I was too focused on long-tail failure modes. The real month-3 tell is simpler than I made it. Does weekly active usage trend up or down after the first cohort of 5 paying customers? Everything else is noise until that metric speaks.

### product-cynic

Concession: I teased "will you still care in six weeks" but that is the actual filter, not a joke. Founders who stay married to a bad idea are rarer than founders who bounce early. If someone is asking this question openly, they have already stayed with it longer than most. Give them the plan.

---

## Synthesis by product-boss

Spend 30 days validating before you write a line of product code. That is the answer.

Here is why. A SaaS that starts with code dies most often from positioning and distribution, not engineering. The cheapest day to pivot is today, before any asset is built. A technical founder's instinct is to ship; the right discipline is to ship the cheapest possible test of demand first.

The 30-day plan, concrete:

Week 1. Write a one-page ideal customer profile, named and measurable. Not "professionals", not "freelancers", but "French freelance developers who invoice at least 3 clients per month and use Indy today". Write a one-page rough unit economics model: target price, expected CAC by channel, target retention, lifetime value. If either document does not hold on paper, iterate the idea before anything else. No guilt in stopping here, it is the cheapest iteration you will ever run.

Week 2. Build a landing page with real pricing and a "notify me" form. Spend 300 dollars on targeted ads pointed at the ICP. In parallel, manually reach out to 20 people who match the ICP and ask for 20-minute interviews about the problem, not the solution. Open questions, not pitch.

Week 3. From the interviews, capture the 3 most repeated problems in the customer's own words. Repetition is the signal. If every interview went a different direction, the ICP is wrong, go back to week 1.

Week 4. Build the smallest possible thing that solves the top problem. One feature. Deploy it. Charge 10 to 20 dollars a month. Goal: 5 paying customers in 30 days. Five paying strangers means market. Four or fewer means iterate or pivot.

Open questions you need to answer:

- Do you already have any captive audience (newsletter, community, customer base)? If yes, skip most of the validation and ship to them directly. Validation is for founders without distribution.
- What unsexy segment do you know deeply? Pick a narrow market you understand (accountants, plumbers, niche SaaS vertical) over a crowded one everyone is fighting over.
- Are you ready to answer support tickets for three to five years? Or is this an exit play? The plan diverges depending on the answer.

Confidence: medium. The plan is sound for a founder without distribution, and the validation sequence is proven. What would raise it to high: a named audience you already have, or a specific vertical you know inside out. What would kill it: you cannot articulate in one sentence why YOU are the right person to build this. That gap widens over time, it does not close.
